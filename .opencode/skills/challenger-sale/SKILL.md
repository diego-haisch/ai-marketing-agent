# Challenger Sale — ApplyChain Adaptation

Use this skill when writing any commercial message, designing a follow-up strategy, or planning outreach for ApplyChain.

## Core Principles (Teach → Tailor → Take Control)

### 1. TEACH — Lead with Insight, Not with Product

The Challenger opens by teaching the prospect something they don't know about their own business. **Never start by talking about ApplyChain.** Start by reframing their problem.

**ApplyChain Insight Template:**
> "Most fashion SMEs think their stock problem is about buying too much. But the real issue is that they're buying blind — they make the same initial buy decisions as companies 10x their size, but without the forecasting tools. The result? 20-30% of their working capital is stuck in the wrong product, in the wrong location."

**Insight properties (must have all 4):**
1. **Leads to your strengths** — the insight points to AI forecasting as the solution only you can provide
2. **Challenges assumptions** — "Your problem isn't bad buying, it's buying without data"
3. **Catalyzes action** — creates urgency ("every month without this, you're losing margin")
4. **Scales** — same insight applies to Punt Roma, Koroshi, EseOese, Surkana, etc.

### 2. TAILOR — Map the Message to the Stakeholder

Different stakeholders care about different things. **Never send the same message to a CEO and an Operations Director.**

| Stakeholder | Their Priority | Message Angle |
|---|---|---|
| **CEO/Owner** | Cash flow, growth, competitiveness | "Free up working capital tied in wrong stock" |
| **Operations Director** | Efficiency, process, team | "Reduce manual planning, give your team better tools" |
| **Head of Buying/Purchasing** | Accuracy, reduced risk | "Buy with confidence, reduce markdowns" |
| **CFO** | Working capital, margin | "20-30% of stock is in the wrong place — that's cash frozen" |
| **IT Director** | Integration, simplicity | "Works with your existing systems, no big implementation" |

**Tailoring checklist:**
- [ ] Do I know the stakeholder's role?
- [ ] Have I framed the problem in their language?
- [ ] Am I speaking to their personal motivation (not just company need)?

### 3. TAKE CONTROL — Drive the Conversation Forward

The Challenger doesn't wait for the prospect to decide when to meet. They propose the next step with confidence.

**Take Control rules:**
- Propose a specific time, not "whenever works for you"
- Don't discount without asking for something in return (reference, expanded scope, case study rights)
- Push back if the prospect's proposed scope won't deliver value: "If we only look at forecasting, we miss the redistribution piece — and that's where 40% of the waste is"
- Drive the timeline: "Every month of delay costs you roughly X in lost margin"

## The 6-Act Challenger Pitch (ApplyChain Version)

Apply this structure to any outreach message or pitch conversation:

### Act 1: Warmer (credibility)
> "I've spent the last 10 years in fashion operations — Desigual, Mango, Etnia Barcelona — and now I work with SMEs in the sector on exactly these challenges."

### Act 2: Reframe (insight)
> "The conventional wisdom is that forecasting is complex and expensive. But what I'm seeing with companies like yours is that the real cost isn't in the tool — it's in continuing to buy blind while your competitors use AI."

### Act 3: Rational Drowning (quantify)
> "A typical fashion SME with €30M revenue has about 25% of stock in the wrong location. That's €7.5M in working capital generating zero return — or worse, being discounted at 50%."

### Act 4: Emotional Impact (make it personal)
> "I saw this at Etnia Barcelona. We went from guessing to using AI models. Forecast accuracy went from ~60% to 77%. The result wasn't just less overstock — it was less stress for the buying team, better cash flow, and more time to focus on what matters."

### Act 5: A New Way (paint the future)
> "Imagine knowing, before each buying season, exactly how much to order per SKU, per store, per channel. Not perfect — but close enough to reduce overstock by 20-30% and lost sales by 15%."

### Act 6: Your Solution (now introduce ApplyChain)
> "That's what we do at ApplyChain. We build forecasting models and dashboards specifically for fashion SMEs. Fast implementation, no bureaucracy, and results you can measure in weeks."

## Message Templates by Situation

### First Contact (Cold/Warm)

**Structure:** Warmer → Reframe → Rational Drowning → Emotional Impact → CTA

Key rules:
- Max 150-170 words
- No product features — only insight
- CTA is a conversation, not a demo
- Mention reference if available (place naturally, not as opener)

### Follow-up After No Reply (7-10 days)

**Structure:** New insight + gentle pressure

> "Hi [Name], I know you're busy. I just wanted to share a quick thought: [new insight about their specific situation]. Happy to connect if this resonates — no rush."

Key rules:
- Lead with a NEW piece of insight (not just "checking in")
- Keep under 80 words
- Don't apologize for following up

### After Meeting / Interview

**Structure:** Summary + next step

> "Thanks for the conversation. Here's what I took away: [specific problem they mentioned]. Here's how I'd approach it: [brief, concrete plan]. Next step: [specific action with date]."

Key rules:
- Recap the problem in THEIR words
- Propose the next step with a date
- Don't over-promise — be honest about what you can/can't do

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

- **Don't be a Relationship Builder** — the worst performer in complex sales. Being friendly ≠ being useful.
- **Don't start with your product** — start with their problem, reframed.
- **Don't ask "how can I help?"** — tell them what you think the problem is, then ask if you're right.
- **Don't send generic messages** — every message must be tailored to the specific stakeholder and company.
- **Don't apologize for selling** — you're offering value, not asking for a favor.
- **Don't use passive CTAs** — "let me know if interested" is weak. Use "I'll follow up next Tuesday" or "Are you available Thursday at 10?"

## Variables to Ask For

Before writing a message, gather:
- Target company name, sector, size
- Contact person name, role
- Known pain points or challenges
- Stakeholder type (CEO, Ops, Buying, etc.)
- Reference or common connection
- Previous contact history (if any)
- Language preference

## Coaching Yourself (PAUSE Framework)

When reviewing your own messages before sending, use PAUSE:
- **P**reparation: Have I researched the company and stakeholder?
- **A**ffirmation: Does my opener establish credibility?
- **U**nderstanding: Am I addressing THEIR problem, not mine?
- **S**pecifying: Is my CTA specific and time-bound?
- **E**mbedding: Will they remember this message tomorrow?
